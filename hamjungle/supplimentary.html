Perturbation Gadget Jungle

The rationale behind the use of a perturbation techniques becomes evident through a straightforward illustration. At the heart of all subsequent gadget constructions lies the concept of a `mediator gadget'. As the name implies, a mediator gadget serves as an intermediary in mediating a specific interaction between two particles. To elaborate, consider a rudimentary example involving a two–particle ferromagnetic interaction, where spins tend to align. In this scenario, we can introduce a mediator gadget that engages in an antiferromagnetic interaction with each of the original particles. Assuming, without loss of generality, the original spins are vertically aligned, the net outcome of the mediator particle being in a downward spin state is an effective ferromagnetic interaction between the original particles. Moreover, the two original particles have indirectly interacted.

The intricacies of perturbation gadgets involve complex mathematics. However, a somewhat informal yet instructive overview can be provided as follows: When faced with a pre–existing interaction between particles $i$ and $j$, the introduction of a mediator qubit $c$, serves to produce an effective indirect interaction between $i$ and $j$. This newly formed interaction approximates the original, while preserving the low–energy spectrum.

The gadgets we explore below are further developed in their respective arts. We provide a high–level overview of the construction and possible use cases. The general construction procedure is as follows:
  1. Define the target Hamiltonian term, $H_{\text{targ.}}$
  2. Define the logical target Hamiltonian via some isometry, $\bar{H}_{\text{targ.}}$
  3. Define the perturbation Hamiltonian with is the sum of the unperturbed Hamiltonian and the perturbation term, $\widetilde{H} = H + V$
  4. Use the perturbation theory expansion of the low–energy spectrum truncated to an appropriate order to define the effective Hamiltonian, $H_{\text{eff.}} \approx \widetilde{H}_{-}$
One subtle condition that influences the construction of the gadgets is the allowed interaction terms of the Hamiltonian in question. For example, as it will be shown, to introduce a mediator qubit $c$ that interacts with some system qubit $i$ via the Pauli–$X$ unitary, demands the original Hamiltonian must permit such interactions. More to this point, say a Hamiltonian instance has interactions from the set $\{Y,Z\}$, the an interaction term between a mediator and a system qubit of the form $X_c\otimes Y_j$ is illegal as such a term is not allowed in this system.

It should be noted that there are certain conditions that must be in place in order for the proper use of the perutbation gadgets. These ideas are not developed here, rather we provide an overview of a use case for each gadget. See [references] for further information on the exact conditions of use.

Oliveira and Terhal Gadgets
Given two disjoint subsets of qubits $a$ and $b$, influenced by some $k$--local interactions, succinctly expressed as $A\otimes B$. Let the cardinality of $a$ and $b$ be upper bounded by $\lceil k/2 \rceil$; the goal of the subdivision gadget is to introduce a mediator qubit, $c$, to reduce the degree of locality and approximate the original $k$--local interaction $A\otimes B$, using $(\lceil k/2 \rceil + 1)$--local interactions.

This gadget can be used to reduce $k$--local interactions, for $k>3$, to $3$--local ones via repeated application `in parallel'. This can trivially be seen with a small calculation.
